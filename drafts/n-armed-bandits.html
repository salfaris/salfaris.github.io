The N-armed bandit problem

Posted on: Sat 10 April 2021

Terminology. A one-armed bandit is basically a slot machine. It is called that way because it resembles a bandit with one knife that keeps taking your money away. An \(N\)-armed bandit is essentially a generalization of this resemblance. You can think of it as either \(N\) slot machines or one slot machine with \(N\) levers.

With that out of the way, let's talk about the main idea as it is actually pretty simple.

Layman's setup

Forget slot machines because then your mind starts to imagine all the combinations you can get on every pull of the lever and things get complicated. Just imagine that you have 2 buttons, RED and BLUE, in front of you that you can press. The game now is for you to repeatedly press any of these two buttons. Occasionally, you may get a monetary reward upon a press, say, £2 or £100. Sometimes, things can go against you and you lose money instead.

The goal now is to simply learn which button to press at what time so that over some fixed time period you attain maximal reward. This learning problem is what is called the \(2\)-armed bandit problem; and you can replace \(2\) by an arbitrary positive integer \(N\) to get the idea of the \(N\)-armed bandit problem.

Formal setup

The formal setup extends the layman's setup by some mere math. You still have 2 buttons RED and BLUE, and the game is to repeatedly press them \(T\) times. We call \(T\) the number of time steps, and it is a positive integer.

Notation. Fix \(t \leq T\) to be a time within the time step. Then let \(B_t \in \{ \text{RED}, \text{BLUE} \}\) denotes the button chosen at time \(t\); and let \(r_t\) denotes the payoff of choosing \(B_t\) (at time \(t\)).
